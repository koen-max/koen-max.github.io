Part I

The Foundational Laws of Taoist Eating

Reid is clear from the outset: the overriding principle is balance and harmony. The Four Energies and Five Flavours in food must be harmonised to keep the vital organs in accord. These are not suggestions — they are Reid's non-negotiables.

Law I

Eat sparingly, and you will live a long and healthy life. The basic Taoist measure: eat until you are 70–80% full.

Reid writes: "Mother Nature invariably punishes gluttons with all sorts of misery. The human body simply cannot utilize the enormous quantities and complex combinations of food with which civilized, sedentary man tends to gorge himself daily."
Law II

Chew food thoroughly before swallowing — especially carbohydrates, which require the alkaline enzyme ptyalin in saliva to begin digestion.

Reid cites Gandhi's dictum: "Drink your food and chew your beverages" — meaning solid food must be chewed to liquid form before swallowing.
Law III

Avoid extreme hot and cold temperatures in foods and beverages.

Law IV

Do not drink large quantities of liquid with meals. Doing so dilutes the gastric medium and impairs digestion.

Exception: wine and beer, being fermented, actually assist digestion when taken in moderate quantities with food.
Law V

Select foods that are fresh rather than stale, living rather than dead, and consume them either raw or lightly cooked wherever possible.

Reid quotes Dr McCollum of Johns Hopkins: "Eat nothing unless it will spoil or rot, but eat it before it does." Refined white flour, for example, will not spoil — this is precisely the problem.

Part II

The Science of Food Combining

Reid devotes significant attention to this — and it is perhaps the most immediately actionable section of the dietary chapter. The argument is enzymatic: incompatible food combinations send conflicting signals to the digestive system, causing enzymes to impair or neutralize one another.

"All enzymes are specific in their actions, fitting the biochemical reactions for which they are designed as precisely as a key fits a lock. When incompatible enzymes are secreted together, owing to conflicting signals sent by incompatible food combinations, their actions are impaired or neutralized."

— Daniel Reid, Chapter 1

The Master Rules of Combining

Rule 1 — The Central Rule

Never combine concentrated proteins (meat, fish, eggs, cheese) with concentrated starches (bread, potatoes, rice) at the same meal.

Reid calls this "the worst possible combination of foods to mix together." Protein digestion requires an acid medium; starch digestion requires an alkaline one. They cancel each other out — causing fermentation, putrefaction, and bloating.
Rule 2

Never consume sugars and proteins at the same meal.

Rule 3

Never consume meat and milk at the same meal.

Rule 4

Avoid sweet or starchy desserts after large protein or carbohydrate meals.

Rule 5

Eat fruit alone and on an empty stomach — never after a large meal.

Fruit digests rapidly. If trapped behind slower-digesting food, it ferments in the gut, causing gas and toxicity rather than nutrition.
Rule 6

Separate your protein-based meal and your starch-based meal by at least 10–12 hours.

Rule 7

Fats may be combined with carbohydrates, vegetables, and fruits — but should be avoided with concentrated proteins. With light proteins, fat is relatively compatible.

Rule 8

Vegetables combine well with either proteins or starches. They are the great neutrals of the Taoist plate.

Part III

What to Eat — and What to Eliminate

Foods and Substances to Eliminate Entirely

Eliminate

Refined white sugar — Reid calls it "the most hazardous dietary item of all," acidifying the bloodstream faster than anything else. Soft drinks are the extreme expression of this.

Eliminate

Refined white flour and starch — Denatured of all nutritional value; rats fed on it die of starvation. It does not spoil — that alone tells you everything.

Eliminate

Pasteurised and homogenised milk — Reid is explicit: eliminate it entirely from your diet. If raw certified milk is available, treat it as a whole food consumed alone.

Eliminate

Hydrogenated oils and margarine — Avoid all margarines and butter substitutes; all hydrogenated oils are to be removed without exception.

Eliminate

Processed, canned, and preserved food — Shelf life and human health are opposing forces. If it will not rot, it will not nourish.

Minimise Heavily

Grains — Reid notes ancient Taoists consistently advocated bi-gu ("avoid grains"). Grains have been a human staple for only 6,000–7,000 years. Those who avoid them live longer and healthier, according to the Taoist literature.

The Best Fats

Reid is precise here. The best fats are butter and cold-pressed vegetable and nut oils in their fluid state. These include olive, soybean, sunflower, sesame, safflower, corn, and nut oils. Avoid all hydrogenated versions.

Animal Protein — Wild and Natural Only

Reid strongly favours wild fish from seas and rivers over farmed fish. He specifically recommends Japanese sashimi — raw fish — as arguably the most nutritionally potent, enzyme-rich, naturally digestible form of animal protein available.

For chicken: only tu-ji (earth chickens — those left to roam and forage freely) rather than yang-ji (factory-farmed birds). The same principle of wildness and natural diet applies across all animal protein sources.

When consuming cooked meats as the main element of a meal, Reid recommends supplementing with active proteolytic (protein-digesting) enzymes to prevent putrefaction, aid digestion, and speed elimination.

The Five Flavours and Four Energies

Reid explains that Taoists balance their diets according to the energetic and flavour properties of food — not merely nutrients. Each flavour affects the body in distinct ways:

🌶️ Pungent
Ginger, garlic, chili. Neutralise and disperse accumulated toxins.
🌽 Sweet
Corn, peas, dates. Nourish vital energy, harmonise the stomach.
🍋 Sour
Olives, pomegranate. Astringent; stop diarrhoea; solidify digestive contents.
🌿 Bitter
Rhubarb, bitter melon. Dry excess dampness, balance the system, purge bowels.
🌊 Salty
Kelp. Soften and moisten tissues; facilitate bowel movements.

The Four Energies — hot, warm, cool, cold — define the energetic intensity of food when digested. Yang foods (beef, mutton, chicken, alcohol, mango, chillies) generate warmth. Taoists balance these against cooler, yin foods and strictly avoid combinations that conflict energetically.

Part IV

A Daily Rhythm — How to Structure Your Eating

Reid does not prescribe a rigid meal plan, but the principles allow us to construct an ideal daily structure. The protein-starch separation rule of 10–12 hours is the structural anchor.

On Waking — Empty Stomach

Fruit or Fresh Juice Only

Fruit is best consumed alone, on a completely empty stomach, in the morning. It digests quickly and cleanly when unimpeded. Fresh juices (especially carrot juice — mentioned repeatedly by Reid) are deeply valued. Nothing else should accompany it.

Mid-Morning / Midday

The Starch Meal

A meal centred on whole, unrefined carbohydrates — grains (if consumed at all, whole and naturally prepared), root vegetables, legumes — combined with abundant vegetables. No animal protein at this meal. Chew every mouthful thoroughly. Eat only to 70–80% satiety.

Afternoon

The Gap

Allow 10–12 hours between the starch meal and any protein meal. This is Reid's hard rule. Snacking is not the Taoist way — each meal should be an intentional, properly combined event, not a continuous grazing process.

Evening

The Protein Meal

A meal centred on quality animal protein — preferably wild fish (ideally raw as sashimi, or lightly cooked), free-range fowl, or eggs — combined generously with vegetables. No starch at this meal. Supplement cooked meat meals with proteolytic enzymes. A moderate amount of fermented wine or beer is acceptable and aids digestion.

After Meals

No Fruit, No Sweet Desserts

Fruit eaten after a large meal ferments. Sweet desserts after protein or starch meals impair digestion. The meal ends with the savoury — fruit is for the morning, alone.

Part V

Fasting — The Taoist Reset

Reid treats fasting not as a dietary option but as a fundamental practice of health maintenance — an ancient Oriental health ritual still practiced in enlightened circles in the East.

The Weekly Mini-Fast

Reid recommends a once-a-week mini-fast of 24–36 hours as a regular health practice. This is the minimum threshold for the body to begin genuine detoxification — it takes at least 24 hours before the body commences pouring toxins into the bloodstream for elimination.

  • Fast for 24–36 hours, ideally weekly.
  • During the fast, the body must be allowed to complete its detox cycle — stopping too soon means toxins are re-circulated rather than eliminated.
  • Before any fast can begin working, you must abstain from food for at least 12 hours to drop blood sugar low enough for the body to switch from glucose to fat for fuel.
  • The 2–3 days of cathartic discomfort during a longer fast are essential and must be endured — this is when true elimination occurs.

Reid is direct: fasting is not starvation. It is the body's ancient self-repair mechanism. Modern eating habits that prevent the body from ever entering a fasted state are, in his view, one of the primary causes of chronic disease.

Part VI

Water, Beverages, and What to Drink

Reid's index references "pints daily" — suggesting generous daily water intake is foundational. The key rules around beverages are:

Permitted with meals

Fermented wine and beer in moderate quantities — these are enzymatically active and actually assist digestion when taken with food.

Avoid with meals

Large quantities of any other beverage — water included — as this dilutes gastric juices and impairs digestion directly.

Eliminate entirely

Soft drinks — Reid calls them "Acid Bombs." One serving delivers acid equivalent to 9 teaspoons of refined sugar, instantly destabilising blood chemistry.

Ideal morning drink

Fresh carrot juice and other fresh vegetable or fruit juices consumed alone on an empty stomach — referenced repeatedly throughout the book.

Part VII

Know Your Metabolic Type

Reid introduces an important nuance: not all people have the same metabolic profile, and the ideal diet differs accordingly.

Type Characteristics Dietary Emphasis
Fast Oxidiser Feels depleted without regular protein and fat; sugars and starches cause nervousness or energy crashes Steady supply of protein and fat; restrict sugars and starches significantly
Slow Oxidiser Thrives on carbohydrates; heavy animal protein leaves them feeling sluggish Emphasise vegetables, fruits, and whole starches; lighter protein
Balanced Metaboliser The majority — neither extreme applies; neither protein nor starch alone causes dysfunction Select wholesome foods from both categories and combine them correctly — the food combining rules are the primary concern

Reid notes that most people in the West have balanced metabolisms — but that civilisation has obscured this through chronic exposure to refined and improperly combined food. Regardless of type, food combining rules apply universally.

The Complete Taoist Eating Protocol — At a Glance

  1. Quantity Eat only to 70–80% fullness at every meal, without exception. Overeating is the root of misery.
  2. Chewing Chew every mouthful until liquid — especially carbohydrates. This is where carbohydrate digestion begins.
  3. Freshness Eat only living food. If it will not rot, do not eat it.
  4. Food Combining Rule 1 Never protein and starch at the same meal. Separate them by 10–12 hours minimum.
  5. Food Combining Rule 2 Never protein and sugar together. Never meat and milk together.
  6. Fruit Always alone, always on an empty stomach, always in the morning. Never after a meal.
  7. Fasting Fast for 24–36 hours once a week. It is not optional — it is the body's reset mechanism.
  8. Beverages Drink minimally during meals. No soft drinks. Fermented wine or beer in moderation is compatible with food.
  9. Animal Protein Wild only. Raw fish (sashimi) is the pinnacle. Free-range, naturally fed poultry. Supplement cooked meat with proteolytic enzymes.
  10. Eliminate Refined sugar. Refined white flour. Pasteurised milk. Hydrogenated oils. Processed and preserved food. Soft drinks.
  11. Fats Butter and cold-pressed liquid oils only. No margarines. No hydrogenated anything.
  12. Flavour Balance Consciously include the Five Flavours — pungent (garlic, ginger), sweet, sour, bitter, salty — in balanced proportion across your diet.
